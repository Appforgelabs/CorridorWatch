📖 How the Corridor Method Works

The Corridor Method is AJ's proprietary stock valuation framework, used to determine relative attractiveness of a stock's entry point based on historically-observed forward earnings multiples.

  • Forward P/E Multiple: At each point in time, AJ calculates the implied forward P/E = stock price ÷ next-twelve-months (NTM) EPS estimate.
  • 1-Year Corridor: Takes the median observed forward P/E over the last 252 trading days (1 year). The "fair value" price = median multiple × current NTM EPS. Upper band = (median + 1.5σ) × EPS. Lower band = (median − 1.5σ) × EPS.
  • 90-Day Corridor: Same methodology, but uses only the last 90 trading days — more sensitive to recent sentiment shifts.
  • Reading the signal: Stock trading above upper band = expensive relative to its own history = least attractive entry. Stock trading below lower band = cheap relative to its own history = most attractive entry.
  • Key insight: The corridor doesn't say what a stock is "worth" in absolute terms — it says whether the market is pricing it richly or cheaply relative to how it has historically been priced. Sentiment matters as much as fundamentals.
  • Long-term upside is a separate analysis: combines earnings growth rate × likely future multiple expansion. A stock can be a poor near-term entry (above corridor) but still have huge long-term upside (PLTR).
  • PEG (Price/Earnings-to-Growth): Identifies the cheapest growth — stocks where you pay the least per unit of earnings growth expected. Oracle and AMD consistently screen cheapest here.

AJ's edge: more accurate EPS estimates than Wall Street consensus (3% error vs 7% on Apple; near-perfect on Oracle). More accurate estimates → more accurate corridor bounds → better entry signals.
